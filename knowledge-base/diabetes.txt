DIABETES MELLITUS: COMPREHENSIVE MEDICAL GUIDE

OVERVIEW
Diabetes mellitus is a chronic metabolic disorder characterized by elevated blood glucose levels (hyperglycemia) resulting from defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both. The condition affects how the body processes blood sugar (glucose), which is vital for energy.

TYPES OF DIABETES

Type 1 Diabetes
- Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells
- Absolute insulin deficiency
- Typically diagnosed in children and young adults
- Requires lifelong insulin therapy
- Accounts for 5-10% of all diabetes cases
- Symptoms develop rapidly: excessive thirst, frequent urination, unexplained weight loss, fatigue

Type 2 Diabetes
- Insulin resistance with relative insulin deficiency
- Most common form (90-95% of cases)
- Associated with obesity, sedentary lifestyle, and genetic factors
- Often develops gradually in adults over 45
- May be managed with lifestyle changes, oral medications, or insulin
- Symptoms develop slowly and may be subtle initially

Gestational Diabetes
- Develops during pregnancy
- Usually resolves after delivery
- Increases risk of Type 2 diabetes later in life
- Requires careful monitoring and management during pregnancy

Prediabetes
- Blood glucose levels higher than normal but not yet diabetic
- Fasting glucose: 100-125 mg/dL
- HbA1c: 5.7-6.4%
- Reversible with lifestyle interventions

DIAGNOSTIC CRITERIA

Fasting Plasma Glucose (FPG)
- Normal: Less than 100 mg/dL
- Prediabetes: 100-125 mg/dL
- Diabetes: 126 mg/dL or higher (on two separate tests)

Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT)
- Normal: Less than 140 mg/dL (2 hours after 75g glucose load)
- Prediabetes: 140-199 mg/dL
- Diabetes: 200 mg/dL or higher

Hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c)
- Normal: Less than 5.7%
- Prediabetes: 5.7-6.4%
- Diabetes: 6.5% or higher
- Reflects average blood glucose over past 2-3 months

Random Plasma Glucose
- Diabetes: 200 mg/dL or higher with classic symptoms

SYMPTOMS AND COMPLICATIONS

Early Symptoms
- Polyuria (frequent urination)
- Polydipsia (excessive thirst)
- Polyphagia (increased hunger)
- Unexplained weight loss
- Fatigue and weakness
- Blurred vision
- Slow-healing wounds
- Frequent infections

Acute Complications
- Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA): Life-threatening, primarily Type 1
- Hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state (HHS): Severe dehydration, Type 2
- Hypoglycemia: Low blood sugar from medication or insulin

Chronic Complications
- Cardiovascular disease: Heart attack, stroke, atherosclerosis
- Nephropathy: Kidney damage and failure
- Retinopathy: Vision loss and blindness
- Neuropathy: Nerve damage causing pain, numbness, tingling
- Diabetic foot: Ulcers, infections, potential amputation
- Skin conditions: Infections, poor wound healing
- Dental problems: Gum disease, tooth loss
- Cognitive impairment: Increased dementia risk

MANAGEMENT AND TREATMENT

Blood Glucose Monitoring
- Self-monitoring of blood glucose (SMBG)
- Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM)
- Target ranges vary by individual
- Typical targets: Fasting 80-130 mg/dL, Post-meal <180 mg/dL

Medications for Type 2 Diabetes
- Metformin: First-line therapy, reduces glucose production
- Sulfonylureas: Stimulate insulin secretion
- DPP-4 inhibitors: Enhance incretin hormones
- GLP-1 receptor agonists: Increase insulin, decrease glucagon
- SGLT2 inhibitors: Increase glucose excretion in urine
- Insulin therapy: When oral medications insufficient

Insulin Therapy
- Rapid-acting: Lispro, aspart, glulisine (onset 5-15 min)
- Short-acting: Regular insulin (onset 30-60 min)
- Intermediate-acting: NPH (onset 1-2 hours)
- Long-acting: Glargine, detemir, degludec (onset 1-2 hours, duration 24+ hours)
- Premixed combinations available

Lifestyle Modifications
- Medical nutrition therapy: Carbohydrate counting, portion control
- Regular physical activity: 150 minutes/week moderate exercise
- Weight management: 5-10% weight loss improves control
- Smoking cessation
- Stress management
- Adequate sleep (7-9 hours)

DIETARY RECOMMENDATIONS

Carbohydrate Management
- Choose complex carbohydrates over simple sugars
- Monitor total carbohydrate intake
- Glycemic index awareness
- Consistent meal timing

Recommended Foods
- Non-starchy vegetables: Leafy greens, broccoli, peppers
- Whole grains: Brown rice, quinoa, oats
- Lean proteins: Fish, poultry, legumes, tofu
- Healthy fats: Nuts, seeds, avocado, olive oil
- Low-fat dairy products

Foods to Limit
- Sugary beverages and desserts
- Refined carbohydrates: White bread, pastries
- Processed foods high in sodium
- Saturated and trans fats
- Excessive alcohol

MONITORING AND FOLLOW-UP

Regular Testing
- HbA1c: Every 3 months if not at goal, every 6 months if stable
- Blood pressure: Each visit
- Lipid profile: Annually
- Kidney function: Annually (serum creatinine, urine albumin)
- Eye examination: Annually
- Foot examination: Each visit
- Dental check-ups: Every 6 months

Target Goals
- HbA1c: Generally <7% (individualized based on patient factors)
- Blood pressure: <140/90 mmHg (or <130/80 for some patients)
- LDL cholesterol: <100 mg/dL (or <70 for high cardiovascular risk)
- Triglycerides: <150 mg/dL
- HDL cholesterol: >40 mg/dL (men), >50 mg/dL (women)

PREVENTION STRATEGIES

For Type 2 Diabetes
- Maintain healthy weight (BMI 18.5-24.9)
- Regular physical activity
- Balanced diet rich in fiber, low in processed foods
- Limit alcohol consumption
- Regular health screenings for those at risk
- Manage stress effectively

Risk Factors
- Family history of diabetes
- Overweight or obesity
- Age 45 or older
- Sedentary lifestyle
- History of gestational diabetes
- Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)
- High blood pressure
- Abnormal cholesterol levels
- Certain ethnicities (African American, Hispanic, Native American, Asian American)

SPECIAL POPULATIONS

Children and Adolescents
- Type 1 diabetes more common
- Increasing Type 2 diabetes rates due to obesity
- Family involvement crucial
- School management plans needed
- Transition to adult care planning

Pregnancy
- Preconception counseling important
- Tight glucose control reduces complications
- Increased monitoring frequency
- Medication adjustments may be needed
- Postpartum screening for gestational diabetes

Elderly
- Individualized treatment goals
- Avoid hypoglycemia
- Consider comorbidities
- Simplified medication regimens
- Caregiver involvement

EMERGENCY SITUATIONS

Hypoglycemia (Low Blood Sugar)
- Symptoms: Shakiness, sweating, confusion, rapid heartbeat
- Treatment: 15-20g fast-acting carbohydrate (glucose tablets, juice)
- Recheck in 15 minutes
- Severe cases: Glucagon injection or emergency medical care

Hyperglycemia (High Blood Sugar)
- Symptoms: Increased thirst, frequent urination, fatigue, blurred vision
- Check for ketones if glucose >240 mg/dL
- Increase fluid intake
- Follow sick day management plan
- Seek medical care if persistent or ketones present

PATIENT EDUCATION

Self-Management Skills
- Blood glucose monitoring technique
- Medication administration
- Carbohydrate counting
- Recognition and treatment of hypo/hyperglycemia
- Sick day management
- Foot care
- When to seek medical attention

Support Resources
- Diabetes educators
- Registered dietitians
- Support groups
- Online resources and apps
- Community programs

PROGNOSIS

With proper management, people with diabetes can lead full, healthy lives. Key factors for success include:
- Consistent blood glucose control
- Regular medical follow-up
- Adherence to treatment plan
- Healthy lifestyle habits
- Early detection and treatment of complications
- Patient education and empowerment
- Strong support system

Uncontrolled diabetes significantly increases risk of serious complications and reduces life expectancy. However, intensive management and modern treatments have dramatically improved outcomes for people with diabetes.
