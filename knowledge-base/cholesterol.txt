CHOLESTEROL AND LIPID DISORDERS: COMPREHENSIVE MEDICAL GUIDE

OVERVIEW
Cholesterol is a waxy, fat-like substance essential for building cell membranes, producing hormones, and synthesizing vitamin D. However, elevated cholesterol levels, particularly LDL cholesterol, significantly increase the risk of cardiovascular disease, heart attack, and stroke. Understanding and managing cholesterol is crucial for cardiovascular health.

TYPES OF CHOLESTEROL AND LIPOPROTEINS

Low-Density Lipoprotein (LDL) Cholesterol
- "Bad cholesterol"
- Carries cholesterol from liver to cells
- Excess LDL deposits in artery walls
- Primary target for cholesterol-lowering therapy
- Major risk factor for atherosclerosis

High-Density Lipoprotein (HDL) Cholesterol
- "Good cholesterol"
- Removes cholesterol from arteries
- Transports cholesterol back to liver for disposal
- Higher levels protective against heart disease
- Levels >60 mg/dL reduce cardiovascular risk

Very Low-Density Lipoprotein (VLDL)
- Carries triglycerides in blood
- Converted to LDL in bloodstream
- Contributes to plaque buildup

Triglycerides
- Most common type of fat in body
- Store excess energy from diet
- Elevated levels increase cardiovascular risk
- Often elevated with obesity, diabetes, metabolic syndrome

Total Cholesterol
- Sum of LDL, HDL, and 20% of triglycerides
- General indicator of cardiovascular risk
- Must be interpreted with individual components

CHOLESTEROL LEVELS AND TARGETS

Total Cholesterol
- Desirable: Less than 200 mg/dL
- Borderline high: 200-239 mg/dL
- High: 240 mg/dL or higher

LDL Cholesterol (Primary Target)
- Optimal: Less than 100 mg/dL
- Near optimal: 100-129 mg/dL
- Borderline high: 130-159 mg/dL
- High: 160-189 mg/dL
- Very high: 190 mg/dL or higher

LDL Goals Based on Risk
- Very high risk: <70 mg/dL (or 50% reduction)
- High risk: <100 mg/dL
- Moderate risk: <130 mg/dL
- Low risk: <160 mg/dL

HDL Cholesterol
- Low (increased risk): Less than 40 mg/dL (men), less than 50 mg/dL (women)
- Desirable: 60 mg/dL or higher
- Higher levels provide cardiovascular protection

Triglycerides
- Normal: Less than 150 mg/dL
- Borderline high: 150-199 mg/dL
- High: 200-499 mg/dL
- Very high: 500 mg/dL or higher

Non-HDL Cholesterol
- Total cholesterol minus HDL
- Secondary target after LDL
- Goal: 30 mg/dL higher than LDL goal

CARDIOVASCULAR RISK ASSESSMENT

Risk Factors
- Age: Men ≥45, women ≥55
- Family history: Premature cardiovascular disease
- Smoking: Current or recent
- Hypertension: BP ≥140/90 or on medication
- Low HDL: <40 mg/dL
- Diabetes mellitus
- Chronic kidney disease
- Metabolic syndrome
- Inflammatory conditions

Risk Categories

Very High Risk
- Established cardiovascular disease
- Recent acute coronary syndrome
- Multiple cardiovascular events
- Diabetes with target organ damage

High Risk
- LDL ≥190 mg/dL
- Diabetes (age 40-75)
- 10-year ASCVD risk ≥20%

Moderate Risk
- 10-year ASCVD risk 7.5-19.9%
- Multiple risk factors

Low Risk
- 10-year ASCVD risk <7.5%
- Few risk factors

ASCVD Risk Calculator
- Estimates 10-year risk of heart attack or stroke
- Considers: Age, sex, race, cholesterol, BP, diabetes, smoking
- Guides treatment decisions
- Available online and in clinical tools

CAUSES OF HIGH CHOLESTEROL

Primary (Genetic) Causes

Familial Hypercholesterolemia
- Inherited genetic disorder
- Very high LDL from birth
- Increased early cardiovascular disease risk
- Requires aggressive treatment
- Affects 1 in 250 people

Familial Combined Hyperlipidemia
- Elevated LDL and triglycerides
- Common genetic disorder
- Increased cardiovascular risk

Familial Hypertriglyceridemia
- Very high triglycerides
- Risk of pancreatitis if >500 mg/dL
- Moderate cardiovascular risk increase

Secondary Causes
- Unhealthy diet: High saturated fat, trans fat
- Obesity: Especially abdominal obesity
- Physical inactivity: Sedentary lifestyle
- Smoking: Lowers HDL, damages arteries
- Diabetes: Often associated with dyslipidemia
- Hypothyroidism: Slows cholesterol metabolism
- Kidney disease: Affects lipid metabolism
- Liver disease: Impairs cholesterol processing
- Medications: Steroids, beta-blockers, thiazides
- Excessive alcohol: Raises triglycerides

COMPLICATIONS OF HIGH CHOLESTEROL

Atherosclerosis
- Plaque buildup in arteries
- Narrows and hardens arteries
- Reduces blood flow
- Foundation for cardiovascular disease

Coronary Artery Disease
- Plaque in heart arteries
- Chest pain (angina)
- Heart attack risk
- Leading cause of death

Stroke
- Plaque in brain arteries
- Reduced blood flow to brain
- Ischemic or hemorrhagic stroke
- Permanent disability or death

Peripheral Artery Disease
- Plaque in leg arteries
- Leg pain with walking (claudication)
- Poor wound healing
- Amputation risk

Carotid Artery Disease
- Plaque in neck arteries
- Stroke risk
- Transient ischemic attacks (TIAs)

Aortic Aneurysm
- Weakening of aorta wall
- Risk of rupture
- Life-threatening emergency

DIAGNOSIS AND SCREENING

Lipid Panel (Fasting)
- Total cholesterol
- LDL cholesterol
- HDL cholesterol
- Triglycerides
- Non-HDL cholesterol
- Calculated ratios

Screening Recommendations
- Adults 20+: Every 4-6 years if normal
- High risk: More frequent monitoring
- Children: Screening at age 9-11 and 17-21
- Family history of early heart disease: Earlier screening

Advanced Lipid Testing
- Apolipoprotein B (ApoB)
- Lipoprotein(a) [Lp(a)]
- LDL particle number
- LDL particle size
- Used for risk refinement

Additional Tests
- High-sensitivity C-reactive protein (hs-CRP)
- Coronary calcium score
- Carotid intima-media thickness
- Ankle-brachial index

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS

Therapeutic Lifestyle Changes (TLC) Diet

Dietary Fats
- Limit saturated fat: <7% of calories
- Minimize trans fat: <1% of calories
- Increase unsaturated fats: Olive oil, nuts, avocados
- Omega-3 fatty acids: Fatty fish 2x/week

Foods to Emphasize
- Fruits and vegetables: 5+ servings daily
- Whole grains: Oats, barley, brown rice
- Legumes: Beans, lentils, peas
- Nuts and seeds: Almonds, walnuts, flaxseeds
- Fatty fish: Salmon, mackerel, sardines
- Plant sterols/stanols: 2g daily (fortified foods)
- Soluble fiber: 10-25g daily

Foods to Limit
- Red meat and processed meats
- Full-fat dairy products
- Fried foods
- Baked goods with trans fats
- Sugary foods and beverages
- Excessive alcohol

Dietary Patterns
- Mediterranean diet: Proven cardiovascular benefits
- DASH diet: Lowers BP and cholesterol
- Portfolio diet: Combines cholesterol-lowering foods
- Plant-based diets: Reduce LDL significantly

Physical Activity
- 150 minutes/week moderate aerobic exercise
- Or 75 minutes/week vigorous exercise
- Resistance training 2 days/week
- Increases HDL by 5-10%
- Lowers triglycerides by 20-30%
- Activities: Walking, jogging, cycling, swimming

Weight Management
- Lose 5-10% body weight if overweight
- Each 10 lb lost reduces LDL by 5-8 mg/dL
- Reduces triglycerides significantly
- Increases HDL
- BMI goal: 18.5-24.9 kg/m²

Smoking Cessation
- Improves HDL levels
- Reduces cardiovascular risk
- Benefits begin immediately
- Comprehensive support programs recommended

Alcohol Moderation
- Moderate intake may increase HDL
- Men: ≤2 drinks/day
- Women: ≤1 drink/day
- Excessive intake raises triglycerides

MEDICATIONS

Statins (HMG-CoA Reductase Inhibitors)
- First-line therapy for high LDL
- Examples: Atorvastatin, rosuvastatin, simvastatin
- Mechanism: Block cholesterol synthesis in liver
- LDL reduction: 30-50%
- Also reduce cardiovascular events
- Side effects: Muscle pain, elevated liver enzymes, diabetes risk
- Monitoring: Liver function, muscle symptoms

Statin Intensity
- High-intensity: Lowers LDL ≥50% (atorvastatin 40-80mg, rosuvastatin 20-40mg)
- Moderate-intensity: Lowers LDL 30-49%
- Low-intensity: Lowers LDL <30%

Ezetimibe
- Blocks cholesterol absorption in intestine
- LDL reduction: 15-20%
- Often combined with statin
- Minimal side effects
- Proven cardiovascular benefit

PCSK9 Inhibitors
- Examples: Evolocumab, alirocumab
- Injectable medications (every 2-4 weeks)
- LDL reduction: 50-60%
- For very high risk or statin intolerance
- Expensive but highly effective
- Proven cardiovascular benefit

Bile Acid Sequestrants
- Examples: Cholestyramine, colesevelam
- Bind bile acids in intestine
- LDL reduction: 15-30%
- Side effects: GI symptoms, constipation
- May interfere with other medications

Fibrates
- Examples: Fenofibrate, gemfibrozil
- Primarily lower triglycerides (30-50%)
- Modestly increase HDL
- Used for hypertriglyceridemia
- Side effects: GI symptoms, gallstones
- Caution with statins (muscle risk)

Niacin (Nicotinic Acid)
- Lowers LDL and triglycerides
- Raises HDL significantly
- Side effects: Flushing, itching, liver toxicity
- Less commonly used now
- Extended-release formulations available

Omega-3 Fatty Acids
- Prescription: Icosapent ethyl, omega-3-acid ethyl esters
- Lower triglycerides significantly
- Cardiovascular benefit shown with icosapent ethyl
- Dose: 2-4g daily
- Generally well tolerated

Bempedoic Acid
- Newer oral medication
- Lowers LDL by 15-25%
- Alternative for statin intolerance
- Taken once daily
- Fewer muscle side effects

Inclisiran
- siRNA therapy targeting PCSK9
- Injectable every 6 months
- LDL reduction: 50%
- Convenient dosing schedule
- Newer option for high-risk patients

TREATMENT APPROACH

Primary Prevention
- Lifestyle modifications for all
- Statin if 10-year ASCVD risk ≥7.5%
- Statin if LDL ≥190 mg/dL
- Statin for diabetes (age 40-75)
- Consider statin for moderate risk with risk enhancers

Secondary Prevention
- High-intensity statin for all with ASCVD
- Add ezetimibe if LDL not at goal
- Add PCSK9 inhibitor if still not at goal
- Aggressive LDL lowering (<70 mg/dL)
- Lifestyle modifications continue

Hypertriglyceridemia
- Lifestyle modifications first
- Fibrate or omega-3 if >500 mg/dL (pancreatitis risk)
- Address secondary causes
- Control diabetes and weight

Low HDL
- Focus on lifestyle modifications
- Exercise most effective
- Weight loss if overweight
- Smoking cessation
- No specific medications proven beneficial

MONITORING AND FOLLOW-UP

Initial Monitoring
- Lipid panel 4-12 weeks after starting medication
- Assess response and tolerability
- Adjust dose or add medications as needed

Ongoing Monitoring
- Lipid panel every 3-12 months once stable
- More frequent if not at goal
- Monitor for side effects
- Liver function tests if indicated
- Creatine kinase if muscle symptoms

Adherence
- Medication adherence crucial
- Address barriers: Cost, side effects, understanding
- Simplify regimen when possible
- Patient education and engagement

SPECIAL POPULATIONS

Women
- Cardiovascular disease leading cause of death
- Risk increases after menopause
- Pregnancy: Avoid statins
- Preconception planning important

Children and Adolescents
- Screen if family history of early CVD or high cholesterol
- Lifestyle modifications primary approach
- Medications if LDL ≥190 mg/dL or familial hypercholesterolemia
- Statins safe in children ≥10 years when indicated

Elderly
- High prevalence of elevated cholesterol
- Proven benefit of statins even in elderly
- Consider life expectancy and comorbidities
- Start low, go slow
- Monitor for drug interactions

Diabetes
- Often have dyslipidemia (high triglycerides, low HDL)
- Statin recommended for most
- Aggressive LDL lowering
- Address all cardiovascular risk factors

PREVENTION

Primary Prevention
- Healthy diet from childhood
- Regular physical activity
- Maintain healthy weight
- Avoid smoking
- Limit alcohol
- Regular screening
- Know family history

Secondary Prevention
- Aggressive risk factor modification
- Medication adherence
- Regular monitoring
- Cardiac rehabilitation
- Comprehensive lifestyle changes

PROGNOSIS

With proper management:
- Significant reduction in cardiovascular events
- 25-35% reduction in heart attacks
- 20-30% reduction in strokes
- Improved quality of life
- Reduced mortality

Each 40 mg/dL reduction in LDL cholesterol reduces cardiovascular risk by approximately 20-25%. Early detection and consistent treatment are essential for optimal outcomes. Lifestyle modifications provide benefits beyond cholesterol lowering, including weight management, blood pressure control, and improved overall health.
