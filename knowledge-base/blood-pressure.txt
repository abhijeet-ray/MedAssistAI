BLOOD PRESSURE AND HYPERTENSION: COMPREHENSIVE MEDICAL GUIDE

OVERVIEW
Blood pressure is the force exerted by circulating blood against the walls of blood vessels. It is one of the vital signs and a critical indicator of cardiovascular health. Hypertension (high blood pressure) is a chronic medical condition that increases the risk of heart disease, stroke, and kidney disease.

UNDERSTANDING BLOOD PRESSURE

Blood Pressure Measurement
- Systolic pressure: Pressure when heart beats (top number)
- Diastolic pressure: Pressure when heart rests between beats (bottom number)
- Measured in millimeters of mercury (mmHg)
- Expressed as systolic/diastolic (e.g., 120/80 mmHg)

Normal Blood Pressure Physiology
- Cardiac output: Amount of blood pumped by heart
- Peripheral vascular resistance: Resistance in blood vessels
- Blood volume: Total amount of blood in circulation
- Regulated by nervous system, kidneys, and hormones

BLOOD PRESSURE CATEGORIES (ACC/AHA Guidelines)

Normal
- Systolic: Less than 120 mmHg
- Diastolic: Less than 80 mmHg
- Maintain healthy lifestyle to prevent progression

Elevated
- Systolic: 120-129 mmHg
- Diastolic: Less than 80 mmHg
- Lifestyle modifications recommended
- Increased risk of developing hypertension

Hypertension Stage 1
- Systolic: 130-139 mmHg OR
- Diastolic: 80-89 mmHg
- Lifestyle changes plus possible medication
- 10-year cardiovascular risk assessment guides treatment

Hypertension Stage 2
- Systolic: 140 mmHg or higher OR
- Diastolic: 90 mmHg or higher
- Lifestyle changes plus medication typically required
- More aggressive treatment approach

Hypertensive Crisis
- Systolic: Higher than 180 mmHg AND/OR
- Diastolic: Higher than 120 mmHg
- Requires immediate medical attention
- May indicate organ damage

TYPES OF HYPERTENSION

Primary (Essential) Hypertension
- No identifiable cause
- Accounts for 90-95% of cases
- Develops gradually over years
- Associated with genetic and lifestyle factors

Secondary Hypertension
- Caused by underlying condition
- 5-10% of cases
- Often appears suddenly with higher readings
- Causes include:
  * Kidney disease
  * Adrenal gland disorders
  * Thyroid problems
  * Sleep apnea
  * Certain medications
  * Congenital blood vessel defects

White Coat Hypertension
- Elevated readings in medical settings only
- Normal readings at home
- May indicate increased cardiovascular risk
- Requires monitoring

Masked Hypertension
- Normal readings in medical settings
- Elevated readings at home or during daily activities
- Associated with increased cardiovascular risk
- Home monitoring important

RISK FACTORS

Non-Modifiable Risk Factors
- Age: Risk increases with age
- Family history: Genetic predisposition
- Race: Higher prevalence in African Americans
- Gender: Men at higher risk until age 64, women after 65

Modifiable Risk Factors
- Obesity: BMI ≥30 kg/m²
- Physical inactivity: Sedentary lifestyle
- Tobacco use: Smoking and secondhand smoke
- Excessive alcohol consumption
- High sodium diet: >2,300 mg/day
- Low potassium intake
- Chronic stress
- Poor sleep quality
- Unhealthy diet: High in saturated fats, low in fruits/vegetables

Medical Conditions
- Diabetes mellitus
- Chronic kidney disease
- Sleep apnea
- High cholesterol
- Metabolic syndrome

SYMPTOMS AND COMPLICATIONS

Symptoms
- Often called "silent killer" - usually no symptoms
- Severe hypertension may cause:
  * Headaches
  * Shortness of breath
  * Nosebleeds
  * Dizziness
  * Chest pain
  * Visual changes
  * Blood in urine

Cardiovascular Complications
- Coronary artery disease
- Heart attack (myocardial infarction)
- Heart failure
- Left ventricular hypertrophy
- Atrial fibrillation
- Sudden cardiac death

Cerebrovascular Complications
- Stroke (ischemic or hemorrhagic)
- Transient ischemic attack (TIA)
- Vascular dementia
- Cognitive impairment

Renal Complications
- Chronic kidney disease
- Kidney failure
- Proteinuria

Other Complications
- Peripheral artery disease
- Aortic aneurysm and dissection
- Retinopathy and vision loss
- Sexual dysfunction
- Metabolic syndrome

DIAGNOSIS AND MONITORING

Proper Blood Pressure Measurement
- Patient seated quietly for 5 minutes
- Feet flat on floor, back supported
- Arm supported at heart level
- Appropriate cuff size
- Two or more readings on separate occasions
- Avoid caffeine, exercise, smoking 30 minutes before

Home Blood Pressure Monitoring
- Validated automated devices recommended
- Measure at same time daily
- Record readings for healthcare provider
- Helps detect white coat and masked hypertension
- Improves treatment adherence

Ambulatory Blood Pressure Monitoring
- 24-hour monitoring with portable device
- Readings every 15-30 minutes
- Most accurate assessment
- Useful for diagnosis confirmation

Diagnostic Evaluation
- Complete medical history
- Physical examination
- Laboratory tests:
  * Complete blood count
  * Basic metabolic panel
  * Lipid profile
  * Urinalysis
  * Electrocardiogram (ECG)
  * Additional tests based on findings

TREATMENT AND MANAGEMENT

Lifestyle Modifications (First-Line for All Patients)

DASH Diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension)
- Rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains
- Low-fat dairy products
- Lean proteins: Fish, poultry, legumes
- Nuts and seeds
- Limited red meat, sweets, sugary beverages
- Can lower BP by 8-14 mmHg

Sodium Reduction
- Limit to <2,300 mg/day (ideal <1,500 mg/day)
- Read food labels
- Avoid processed and restaurant foods
- Use herbs and spices instead of salt
- Can lower BP by 5-6 mmHg

Weight Management
- Lose 5-10% of body weight if overweight
- Each kg lost can reduce BP by 1 mmHg
- BMI goal: 18.5-24.9 kg/m²
- Waist circumference: <40 inches (men), <35 inches (women)

Physical Activity
- 150 minutes/week moderate aerobic exercise
- Or 75 minutes/week vigorous exercise
- Resistance training 2 days/week
- Can lower BP by 5-8 mmHg
- Activities: Walking, jogging, cycling, swimming

Alcohol Moderation
- Men: ≤2 drinks/day
- Women: ≤1 drink/day
- Excessive alcohol raises BP
- Can lower BP by 4 mmHg

Smoking Cessation
- Immediate and long-term benefits
- Reduces cardiovascular risk
- Improves medication effectiveness

Stress Management
- Relaxation techniques
- Meditation and mindfulness
- Yoga and tai chi
- Adequate sleep (7-9 hours)
- Work-life balance

ANTIHYPERTENSIVE MEDICATIONS

First-Line Medications

Thiazide Diuretics
- Examples: Hydrochlorothiazide, chlorthalidone
- Mechanism: Reduce blood volume
- Side effects: Electrolyte imbalances, increased urination
- Monitoring: Potassium, sodium levels

ACE Inhibitors
- Examples: Lisinopril, enalapril, ramipril
- Mechanism: Block angiotensin-converting enzyme
- Side effects: Dry cough, elevated potassium
- Contraindicated in pregnancy
- Monitoring: Kidney function, potassium

Angiotensin II Receptor Blockers (ARBs)
- Examples: Losartan, valsartan, olmesartan
- Mechanism: Block angiotensin II receptors
- Side effects: Similar to ACE inhibitors, no cough
- Alternative to ACE inhibitors
- Monitoring: Kidney function, potassium

Calcium Channel Blockers
- Examples: Amlodipine, diltiazem, verapamil
- Mechanism: Relax blood vessels
- Side effects: Ankle swelling, constipation, headache
- Two types: Dihydropyridines and non-dihydropyridines

Additional Medications

Beta-Blockers
- Examples: Metoprolol, atenolol, carvedilol
- Mechanism: Reduce heart rate and cardiac output
- Used with specific indications (heart failure, post-MI)
- Side effects: Fatigue, cold extremities, bradycardia

Alpha-Blockers
- Examples: Doxazosin, prazosin
- Mechanism: Relax blood vessels
- Often used as add-on therapy
- Side effects: Dizziness, orthostatic hypotension

Direct Vasodilators
- Examples: Hydralazine, minoxidil
- Mechanism: Direct relaxation of blood vessels
- Reserved for resistant hypertension
- Side effects: Fluid retention, tachycardia

Combination Therapy
- Often required for adequate control
- Fixed-dose combinations improve adherence
- Common combinations:
  * ACE inhibitor + diuretic
  * ARB + calcium channel blocker
  * ACE inhibitor + calcium channel blocker

TREATMENT GOALS

General Population
- <130/80 mmHg for most adults
- Individualized based on age, comorbidities, tolerance

Special Populations
- Diabetes: <130/80 mmHg
- Chronic kidney disease: <130/80 mmHg
- Elderly (≥65 years): <130/80 mmHg if tolerated
- Coronary artery disease: <130/80 mmHg

RESISTANT HYPERTENSION

Definition
- BP remains above goal despite:
  * Lifestyle modifications
  * Three antihypertensive medications (including diuretic)
  * Optimal doses

Evaluation
- Confirm true resistance (rule out white coat effect)
- Assess medication adherence
- Review drug interactions
- Screen for secondary causes
- Consider specialist referral

Management
- Add fourth medication (spironolactone often effective)
- Optimize existing regimen
- Address secondary causes
- Consider device-based therapies (renal denervation)

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS

Pregnancy
- Preeclampsia screening
- Safe medications: Methyldopa, labetalol, nifedipine
- Avoid ACE inhibitors and ARBs
- Close monitoring required

Children and Adolescents
- Age, sex, and height-specific percentiles
- Often secondary causes
- Lifestyle modifications emphasized
- Medication if needed

Elderly
- Higher prevalence
- Isolated systolic hypertension common
- Start low, go slow with medications
- Monitor for orthostatic hypotension
- Fall risk assessment

PREVENTION

Primary Prevention
- Maintain healthy weight
- Regular physical activity
- Healthy diet (DASH diet)
- Limit sodium intake
- Moderate alcohol consumption
- Avoid tobacco
- Manage stress
- Regular health screenings

Secondary Prevention
- Early detection through screening
- Prompt treatment of elevated BP
- Prevent progression to complications
- Regular monitoring and follow-up

PATIENT EDUCATION

Self-Management
- Proper home BP monitoring technique
- Medication adherence
- Lifestyle modification strategies
- Recognition of warning signs
- When to seek medical attention

Follow-Up
- Monthly until BP controlled
- Every 3-6 months once stable
- More frequent if medication changes
- Annual comprehensive evaluation

PROGNOSIS

With proper treatment and lifestyle modifications:
- Significant reduction in cardiovascular events
- Decreased risk of stroke by 35-40%
- Decreased risk of heart attack by 20-25%
- Decreased risk of heart failure by 50%
- Improved quality of life
- Reduced mortality

Untreated hypertension leads to progressive organ damage and significantly increased cardiovascular morbidity and mortality. Early detection and consistent management are crucial for optimal outcomes.
